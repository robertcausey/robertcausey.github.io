03_Parasites

Introduction
In this module we will survey parasites, which are eukaryotic organisms, other than fungi, that use a host animal at some point in their life cycle. The group includes protozoa (single celled orgnanisms), helminths (tapeworms, flukes, round worms) and ectoparasites (mites, lice, and insects).

Protozoa
Coccidia
Giardia
Malaria
Sarcocystis
Helminths
Helminths are divided into platyhelminths (cestodes and trematodes) and nematodes (roundworms).
Cestodes
Cestodes have a predator-prey life cycle:
Echinoccus granulosus, tape worm of dogs uses ruminants as prey.
Dipylidium caninum - flea is the prey eaten by the dog or cat

Trematodes
Liver fluke

Nematodes
It may be a gross oversimplification, but round worms may be subdivided into Ascarids (round worms of nneonates and young animals), strongyle types worms (Rhabditia) including Strongylus vulgaris in horses, Ancylostomum caninum in dogs, Haemonchus contortus in sheep), and whipworms Trichuridae.

Ectoparasites
Mites
Mites are very small arachnids which complete their lifecycle on the host. You should also review the Merk Manual mange page.
Otodectes cynotis - dog end cat ear mite (otodectic mange)
Notoedres cati - scabies in cats (notoedric mange)
Sarcoptes scabei var canis - a.k.a scabies - sarcoptic mange in dogs. Contagious and transmissible to humans, but self-limiting i.e. generally it cannot complete life cycle, but there may be exceptions.
Cheyletiella blakei (cats) and Cheyletiella yasguri (dogs) is highly contagious walking dandruff.
Demodex canis - Demodectic mange. Localized and limited disease in puppies, but can be generalized in immunosuppressed puppies and adults. Normally part of the local flora i.e. not contagious.

Ticks
Ticks are arachnids larger than mites in the subclass Acari. They are major transmitters of disease. Part of their life-cycle is off the host. Here is the Merk manual page on ticks. They include:

Dermacentor variabilis American Dog tick, and Dermacento andersonii, the Rocky Mountain wood tick. Both can transmit Rickettsia rickettsii, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF) and other diseases.

Amblyomma americanum the lone star tick also transmits RMSF and Babesia.
transmit Borrelia burgdorferi, causative agent of Lyme disease, Anaplasmosis

Tick paralysis is cause by a potent salivary neurotoxin. Removal of the tick can eliminate the cause. But this is why we want to remove the whole head of the tick along with the body due to the tick's salivary gland being the source of the toxin.

Insects
Insects can be divided into lice, fleas, mosquitoes, midges, flies, and caterpillars.

Lice
Here is the Merck Manual entry lifecycle is on the host
Horses
Hematopinus asini - sucking louse
Damalinia equi - biting louse

Cattle
Linignathus vituli - sucking louse
Bovicola bovis - biting louse

Humans
Phthiriasis pubis - the pubic louse
Pediculus humanus humanus - the body louse.

Fleas
Ctenocephalides canis - dog fleas
Ctenocephalides felis - cat fleas. Sometimes can infest dogs too.
Ctenocephalides spp. are intermediate hosts for Dipylidium caninum

Mosquitoes
Video of mosquito life-cycle, and a video showing wrigglers and tumblers

Aedes, Anopheles, Culex, Culiseta, Psorophora spp. Transmit malaria by protozoa of the Plasmodium genera, and heart worm (Dirofilaria immitis). Major importance of mosquitoes is their role as vectors of other diseases.

Midges
Culicoides spp - bite causes hypersensitivity in horses

Flies
Flies are especially bothersome to horses and cattle. Particular types of fly include:
Gasterophilus intestinalis - horse bot flies. Larvae can infest the horse's stomach.
Stomxys calcitrans - horse stable fly
Tabanus spp - tabanid horse flies lacerating mouth pats, spread Equine Infectious Anemia
Chrysops spp- tabanid deer flies also bite horses.
Hypoderma bovis - cattle warble fly: the larva emerges through the hide of infested cattle.
Myiasis is the name given to maggot infestation in flesh of a living animal or human.

Caterpillars
Not really a parasite but seems the right place to include eastern tent caterpillars. These larvae were implicated in Mare Reproductive Loss Syndrome., an outbreak which occurred in Kentucky in 2001 causing approximately $500 million in losses.
